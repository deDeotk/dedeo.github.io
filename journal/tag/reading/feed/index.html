Read only those books in which leading ideas are expressed at first hand. These are not very numerous. Books repeat one another, water one another down, or contradict one another, and that too is a kind of repetition. On examination we find that discoveries in thought are rare. The old stock or rather the permanent stock of ideas is the best; one must take one’s stand on it in order truly to commune with the intelligence of man, far from petty, stammering, squabbling individualities.
[…]
. . . love the eternal books that express eternal truths.

Antonin Sertillanges, The Intellectual Life: Its Spirit, Conditions, Methods (repr. 1998; Washington DC: Catholic University of America Press, 1987), 150–51.
